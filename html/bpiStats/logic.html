Logic

What stats is

There are many definitions of statistics. Mine is: reasoning under uncertainty.

Suppose polls tell you that 51% of people support a political candidate. Will she win?

Suppose someone offers you a bet that if they throw double-sixes then you give them $100 but if not then they pay you $1. Should you take the bet?

We can't know the answer, nobody can. But some bets are smarter to make than others. In Math we can know things with certainty: 1+1=2 always. Stats uses math, but we use it to describe answers that are probabilistic. Will a coin come up heads? 1/2 probability. The mathematical value is precise but it precisely describes our uncertainty.

Propositions

Seen in this way, statistics is about reasoning. So we need to get good at logic.

... So what is logic? Broadly, it's taking information and drawing conclusions. But we'll focus on a fairly small and manageable part of logic, which deals with propositions, premises, conclusions, and inferences.

Probably every textbook on Logic includes the famous example: "All men are mortal. Socrates is a man. Therefore Socrates is mortal." Every one of these sentences is what we call a proposition. Propositions are sentences that are either true or false. Here are some examples of sentences that are not propositions:

  • What time is it?
  • Yay!
  • Go right.

  2. This is a proposition, but it's a little less clear because right now we don't know whether it's true or false. But the fact remains that it is true or false, we just have to wait to find out which one it is. But it is still true or false and therefore it is a proposition.

Arguments

We use arguments all the time in science. By that I mean we start from some facts, and use those facts to reach conclusions. An example would be:

"Every time you let something go it falls to the ground. So there must be some force pulling things toward the ground."

Notice that "arguments" in this sense don't have to be angry and nobody even has to be disagreeing. What we call "arguments" could also be called "stating our beliefs and giving reasons for those beliefs". The arguments that we study are made up of propositions. Some of these propositions are the conclusion (in the previous example that's the second sentence) or the belief. The other propositions are the reasons, which we call premises.

Test your understanding

In the following, identify the premises and the conclusion. There will always be one conclusion and at least one premise. Hint: You cannot assume that the conclusion always comes last. Also, the conclusion doesn't have to be in its own separate sentence!

  1. The butler killed Watson. It must have been. We know that either the maid or the butler killed Watson. If it was the maid, Watson was poisoned. But Watson wasn't poisoned.
  2. If the earth is round we should be able to see the curvature. We cannot see the curvature, so the earth must be flat. (Note: this is a bad argument, and we'll discuss why in a minute!)

Answers

  1. The conclusion is "The butler killed Watson" because every other sentence is used in support of this claim. The sentence "It must have been." isn't really a premise or conclusion--it's more like a way of saying "And I'll tell you why that's so." But every other sentence in the argument is a premise.
  2. The conclusion is "the earth must be flat" because every other sentence is there to support that claim. Every other proposition is a premise.

Right and wrong arguments

There are two ways that an argument can go wrong: You can start with the wrong premises, or you can use those premises to draw the wrong conclusion. Here's an example of a wrong argument that is flawed because it has false premises:

"If the streets are wet then it must have rained recently. The streets are wet. Therefore it has rained recently.""

The first sentence is not true because the streets could be wet from lawn sprinklers or other causes. Because a premise is not true, the argument may reach a false concluion.

Now here's an argument that contains true premises but doesn't use them well.

"All triangles have a perimeter. Therefore the perimeter of a triangle is \(\pi\)."

The premise is true but it got the conclusion wrong because the premise has nothing (or very little) to do with the conclusion.

An argument that has false premises is said to be unsound and an argument that draws conclusions which aren't justified is called invalid. A good argument then must be both sound and valid.

For each of the following, say whether it's sound and whether it's valid. Hint: Arguments can be sound and valid, unsound and valid, sound and invalid, or unsound and invalid. I give one example of each possibility. Second hint: An argument can have a true conclusion and still be invalid! To be invalid just means that the conclusion isn't justified by the premises.

  1. All cups are green. Socrates is a cup. Therefore Socrates is green.
  2. All men are mortal. Socrates is a man. Therefore Socrates is mortal.
  3. All cups can hold water. Therefore Socrates is mortal.
  4. All cups are green. Therefore Socrates is immortal.

Answers

  1. Unsound, valid. The premise "all cups are green" is false and that makes the argument unsound. But if we accepted this like it's true, and we accept "Socrates is a cup", then it would logically follow that Socrates must be green. That makes the argument valid.
  2. Sound, valid.
  3. Sound, invalid.
  4. Unsound, invalid.

Negation

Propositions are often built using words like "not", "or", "and" and "if" which change the truth-value. For instance you can start from a true sentence "Russia is large" and use negation to get a false sentence "Russia is not large". You likewise can negate a false sentence to get a true one, so negation flips the truth-value of a sentence.

In order to study how these operations work on propositions, we write a proposition with a symbol like P, so that whenever I'm talking about P I'm talking about the proposition that it stands for. That proposition could be anything, like "The high today is 80-degrees." or "Nixon went to China." Often I actually don't care which sentence it is, what I really want to do is study how something like negation acts on any sentence. That's because what I'm really trying to do is study how sentences in general work. So just know that when I use the symbol P I mean some proposition, but I may not have any specific proposition in mind. And what I say about P should be true for every proposition that you might want to analyze.

But since we don't know which proposition we're dealing with, it could be true or false. We diagram these possibilities like you see below.

$$\begin{array}{|c|}\hline P \\\hline T \\\hline F \\\hline\end{array}$$

And then we show how negation acts on each possibility with a table like below:

$$\begin{array}{|c|c|}\hline P & \text{not-}P\\\hline T & F\\\hline F & T\\\hline\end{array}$$

The column on the left is there just to establish when we're considering P to be true or false. The first row is for when P is true and the second row is for when P is false. The column on the right is calculated from the column on the left by flipping the truth-values of P, row-by-row. In this particular case it's very obvious how that works, but as we add more logical operations, the table will help us to manage how complex these calculations get.

Test your understanding

What is the truth-table for a "double-negated" sentence? That is to say, what is the truth-table for not-not-P?

Answer

It's easy enough to reason that the double-negation should have the same truth-table as just plain P. After all, if you flip the truth-values and then flip again, you're right back where you started. In table form we can write this like so.

$$ \begin{array}{|c|c c|} \hline P & \text{not-} & \text{not-P} \\\hline T & \color{red} T & F \\\hline F & \color{red} F & T \\\hline \end{array} $$

On the far right I've just copied the truth-table that we already saw for not-P. Just left of that is the negation of it. Since that is the the actual answer--it is the actual truth-table of the double-negation--I've highlighted it in red.

Conjunction

We often put propositions together using "and". For example "Russia is large and corrupt." This is really two propositions in one. It is the conjunction of "Russia is large" and "Russia is corrupt". To display the logic as much as possible, we separate out these sentences and write their conjunction as "Russia is large and Russia is corrupt."

But now the question is, what is the truth-table for conjunction? One thing is obvious: The table can't be as simple as before, because now we have TWO sentences, not just one. So let's call the first sentence P and the second one Q. It should be clear that if both P and Q are true then the conjunction "P-and-Q" should also be true. This tells us how to treat one row of the truth-table. But this is only for the possiblility where both P and Q are true. What are the other possibilities?

In total, there are four possibilities: P and Q are true, P is true and Q is false, P is false and Q is true, and P and Q are both false. We diagram these possibilities in a table like this:

$$\begin{array}{|c|c|}\hline P & Q\\\hline T & T\\\hline T & F\\\hline F&T \\\hline F&F\\\hline \end{array}$$

Now we want to include in this table the values for "P-and-Q". We already said what goes on the first row:

$$\begin{array}{|c|c|c|}\hline P & Q& P\text{-and-}Q\\\hline T & T&T\\\hline T & F& ?\\\hline F&T & ? \\\hline F&F& ?\\\hline \end{array}$$

We still need the other rows. What if I said "Russia is large and south of Egypt"? Would that sentence be true? It seems half-true, but in logic, we just say that the sentence is false. And the order doesn't matter, so if I said "Russia is south of Egypt and large." that would still be false. So apparently we are putting false values in those rows.

$$\begin{array}{|c|c|c|}\hline P & Q& P\text{-and-}Q\\\hline T & T&T\\\hline T & F& F\\\hline F&T & F \\\hline F&F& ?\\\hline \end{array}$$

Finally, if both sentences are false then the conjunction is a very false sentence and we definitely want a false in that row. So the full table is

$$\begin{array}{|c|c|c|}\hline P & Q& P\text{-and-}Q\\\hline T & T&T\\\hline T & F& F\\\hline F&T & F \\\hline F&F& F\\\hline \end{array}$$

Test your understanding

  1. Build the truth-table for the sentence "not-(P-and-Q)". Although we haven't talked about how to join these operators together, the result should be exactly the same as the truth-table for "and" but with the values flipped.

  2. Build the truth-table for the sentence "(not-P)-and-Q". This time you cannot just negate the values of the conjunction. Instead, first, you flip the values of P and then you use the conjunction rules for these flipped values together with the values under Q.

Answers

The way I like to display these kinds of table calculations is to write a column of truth-values underneath each operation. In this case that means a column under the "and" and a column under the "not". The column under the "and" comes from the conjunction rules that were specified earlier. The column under the "not" is the flip of those values. But really, only the column under the not is the "answer" to this question, so I've highlighted that in red.

  1. $$\begin{array}{|c|c|c c|}\hline P & Q& \text{not-} & (P\text{-and-}Q)\\\hline T & T& \color{red}F & T\\\hline T & F& \color{red}T & F \\\hline F&T & \color{red}T & F \\\hline F&F& \color{red}T & F \\\hline \end{array}$$
  2. I do this similar to the above, except this time we start by flipping the truth-values of P. Since those values originally went TTFF now they become FFTT. AFTER I compute that column, I then compute the "and" column. The values that go into the "and" calculations come from the not-P and from the Q. So for instance, in the first row, not-P takes the value F and Q takes the value T. So we need to compute "and" with an F on the left and a T on the right. You can look at the finished conjunction table to see that this produces an F. That explains what you see on the first row of this answer. $$\begin{array}{|c|c|c c c|}\hline P & Q& (\text{not-}P) & \text{-and-} & Q\\\hline T & T & F & \color{red}F & T \\\hline T & F & F & \color{red}F & F \\\hline F & T & T & \color{red}T & T \\\hline F & F & T & \color{red}F & F \\\hline \end{array}$$

    Notice that the answer for the first question, FTTT, is not the same as the answer for this question, which is FFTF! This shows you that the order in which you do these calculations matters!

Disjunction

Next we talk about the operation that we use when sentences are joined together with "or". Take for example "The prize is behind door one or two." Again we can display the logic by writing this as two propositions "The prize is behind door one or the prize is behind door two." We symbolize this as "P-or-Q". If the "or" proposition is true then one of the sentences is true, but we don't know which one. This already tells us two rows of the truth-table.

$$\begin{array}{|c|c|c|}\hline P & Q& P\text{-or-}Q\\\hline T & T& ?\\\hline T & F& T\\\hline F&T & T \\\hline F&F& ?\\\hline \end{array}$$

And it's pretty easy to believe that the third row should be F. Why? Imagine that you open door one and there's no prize. Bummer. But then just to check, you open door two and there's no prize behind that one either! Now you're pissed. The or-sentence was false! We can fill in the last row:

$$\begin{array}{|c|c|c|}\hline P & Q& P\text{-or-}Q\\\hline T & T& ?\\\hline T & F& T\\\hline F&T & T \\\hline F&F& F\\\hline \end{array}$$

What about the first row? What happens if you have two true sentences? In the prize example that would be very weird because it sounds like there's only one prize. But if there are two prizes, one behind door one, and one behind door two, then is the sentence "The prize is behind door one or door two." a true sentence? I would suggest that technically, it is, even if it seems a little funny to say it like that. So we put T in that row. Therefore the full table is

$$\begin{array}{|c|c|c|}\hline P & Q& P\text{-or-}Q\\\hline T & T& T\\\hline T & F& T\\\hline F&T & T \\\hline F&F& F\\\hline \end{array}$$

Test your understanding

  1. Find the truth-table for not-(P-or-Q).
  2. Find the truth-table for (not-P)-or-Q

Answers

Since you've seen it once before, I won't break the steps down again. Instead I'll just show the final answer.

  1. $$\begin{array}{|c|c|c|}\hline P & Q& \text{not-}(P\text{-or-}Q)\\\hline T & T& F\\\hline T & F& F\\\hline F&T & F \\\hline F&F& T\\\hline \end{array}$$
  2. $$\begin{array}{|c|c|c|}\hline P & Q& (\text{not-}P)\text{-or-}Q\\\hline T & T& T\\\hline T & F& F\\\hline F&T & T \\\hline F&F& T\\\hline \end{array}$$

Conditionals

So far we've looked at operations that have a kind of symmetry to them. For instance, it's probably clear that P-and-Q is basically the same as Q-and-P. Same for "or" propositions. It makes the middle two rows redundant. But there is a very important operation which does not have this symmetry. It is made from sentences that are constructed with the "if ... then ..." construction. For example "If the circle has radius 1 then its area is \(\pi\)." This is a true proposition, which we can symbolize as if-P-then-Q. Think of the various possibilities that come with this sentence: You might be faced with a circle that has radius 1. If you are, then when you measure its area you'll find that it's \(\pi\). This establishes that the first row of the truth-table should contain T, since whenever the two sentences are true the "if-then" is also true.

Let's jump down to the last row real quick: What if you don't have a radius 1 circle, maybe you have a radius 2 circle? When you measure the area you won't get \(\pi\). Is the sentence still true? Yep, it is! Because the sentence didn't try to give any pronouncement about radius 2 circles. The sentence didn't say anything about what to believe when P is false, so nothing like that can count against the truth of the "if-then". We have just justified placing an F on that row. So far the table we've built up is

$$\begin{array}{|c|c|c|}\hline P & Q& \text{if-}P\text{-then-}Q \\\hline T & T& T\\\hline T & F& ?\\\hline F&T & ? \\\hline F&F& T \\\hline \end{array}$$

NOTE: Most people eventually get confused about this. When computing truth-tables involving "if-then" constructions, they often feel like if the two sentences are false, the if-then should come out false. Just remember the example to recall why we got this slightly strange-seeming result. When P is false, the "if-then" sentence hasn't made any claim about the truth of Q. So it can't be wrong in these cases.

The same sort of logic justifies putting a T on the third row. I won't go into details, but it is basically down to the fact that since P is false therefore the if-then cannot come out wrong.

And then there's the second row. What to do when P is true and Q is false? Let's think of the example "If you go right, you will find the grocery store." And suppose that you do in fact go right. But you don't find the grocery store. Well I guess the person telling you directions just lied to you. Put an F there. Now we have the full table.

$$\begin{array}{|c|c|c|}\hline P & Q& \text{if-}P\text{-then-}Q \\\hline T & T& T\\\hline T & F& F\\\hline F&T & T \\\hline F&F& T \\\hline \end{array}$$

Test your understanding

  1. Build the truth-table for if-Q-then-P and compare it with the truth-table for if-P-then-Q.
  2. Build the truth-table for if-(not-P)-then-(not-Q). Does it look like any other truth-table you've seen?

Answers

  1. I'll break this one down, again highlighting the final answer in red. I'll explain how I got the second row since this might help understand how everything else was computed: First, on the second row, Q is false and P is true. Now since Q is on the left and P on the right, that means I use the rule for the if-then table where there's false on the left and true on the right. That means I'm using the third row of that table above to look up what this should be. Apparently that comes out to T, and that's why I have T on the second row below.
    2. $$\begin{array}{|c|c|c c c|}\hline P & Q& \text{if-} Q & \text{-then-} & P \\\hline T & T & T & \color{red}T & T\\\hline T & F & F & \color{red}T & T\\\hline F & T & T & \color{red}F & F\\\hline F & F & F & \color{red}T & F\\\hline \end{array}$$

    The important point about this is that it's NOT the same as the table for if-P-then-Q. This illustrates that "and" and "or" had this kind of symmetry, where you could reverse it. But "if" does not have the symmetry.

  2. $$\begin{array}{|c|c|c c c|}\hline P & Q& \text{if-not-}P & \text{-then-} & \text{not-}Q \\\hline T & T & F & \color{red}T & F\\\hline T & F & F & \color{red}T & T\\\hline F & T & T & \color{red}F & F\\\hline F & F & T & \color{red}T & T\\\hline \end{array}$$

    3. I won't explain here how I got these values. But it is important to remark on the result. The table for if-Q-then-P is exactly the same as the table for if-(not-P)-then-(not-Q)!

Equivalence

What does it mean when two truth-tables come out the same? We've seen this twice now, once for double-negation and once for the two sentences in the exercise above. When the tables come out the same, we call the propositions equivalent. Therefore P is equivalent to not-not-P.

Ok, but what does it mean? It means that the two propositions are said differently but really communicate the same proposition. It's like calling someone Bill versus William: Two names for the same thing.

Test your understanding

Show that not-(P-and-Q) is equivalent to (not-P)-or-(not-Q).

Answer

I won't show the intermediate steps but if you draw a truth-table for (not-P)-and-(not-Q) you should find that it's $$\begin{array}{|c|c|c|}\hline P & Q & (\text{not-}P)\text{-or-}(\text{not-}Q)\\\hline T & T& F\\\hline T & F& T\\\hline F&T & T \\\hline F&F& T\\\hline \end{array}$$ Since this is exactly the flip of the truth-table for P-and-Q then not-(P-and-Q) also has this truth-table. Since they have the same truth-tables they are equivalent.

Conditional components

Especially for conditionals, it's important to get VERY familiar with these equivalences, because these will come up when we start doing hypothesis testing in statistics. Because it's so important, we have a bunch of vocabulary for all this. First, there is vocabulary to help us with the parts of an if-then proposition. For the proposition if-P-then-Q we call P the antecedent and we call Q the consequent. This helps us keep track of which one is the "if" part and which is the "then" part.

Already this introduces some complexity though, because some English sentences don't put the "if" and "then" parts in the order I've been placing them. Take the example proposition "The area is \(\pi\) if the radius is 1." Even though the sentence clauses are swapped around, this still says the same thing as "If the radius is 1 the area is \(\pi\)." The lesson here is that, when reading the English, what tells you the antecedent or the consequent isn't which is on the left or right. It's related to which one comes after the "if" and which comes after the "then".

AND YET even this isn't fully true. This whole system gets messed up when we consider conditionals with the word "only" in them. Consider the sentence "You can go outside and play only if you clean your room." Does this mean "If you clean your room then you can go outside and play"? No, actually it doesn't. It means "If you can go outside and play it's only allowed because you cleaned your room." So it might happen that your mom told you this, and after you cleaned your room she says "You still can't go out, you also need to feed the dog."

In effect what this shows is that the word "only" reverses the position of the antecedent and the consequent. P-only-if-Q is equivalent to if-P-then-Q. Similarly only-if-P-then-Q is equivalent to if-Q-then-P.

  2. Antecedent: "Socrates is mortal", Consequent: "Socrates is a man"
  3. Antecedent: "Socrates is a man", Consequent: "Socrates is mortal"
  4. Antecedent: "Socrates is mortal", Consequent: "Socrates is a man"

Conditional variations

Now that we have the vocabulary to talk about the components of conditionals, we can talk about the important related conditionals. If we have a sentence if-P-then-Q then the sentence if-Q-then-P is called its converse. The sentence if-(not-P)-then-(not-Q) is called its inverse. The sentence if-(not-Q)-then-(not-P) is called its contrapositive.

That is to say, the converse is the sentence where the antecedent and consequent are swapped. The inverse is where the antecedent and consequent are negated. The contrapositive are where the antecedent and consequent are negated and swapped.

Test your understanding

Consider the sentence "If it's raining then the sidewalk is wet." Write its converse, inverse, and contrapositive.

Answers

The converse is "If the sidewalk is wet then it's raining."

The inverse is "If the sidewalk isn't wet then it isn't raining."

The contrapositive is "If it isn't raining then the sidewalk isn't wet."

Conditional Equivalences

We have already seen that, for a conditional if-P-then-Q the inverse is equivalent to the converse. That is to say, although the inverse looks different from the converse, they actually say the same thing. What about the contrapositive? If you write out the truth-table for if-(not-Q)-then-(not-P) then you should find that it has the same truth-table as the original sentence if-P-then-Q. You should test your understanding by demonstrating this to yourself.

The connection with stats

So what's all this got to do with statistics? In fact many statistics classes don't go this deep into logic. So you're lucky because they're missing out!

A common argument style goes like this: If P then Q. Also we know that P. Therefore we can infer Q. Let's do this again but with specific examples for P and Q. "If the radius is 1 then the area is \(\pi\). Also the radius is 1. Therefore the area is \(\pi\)."

The process of going from sentences if-P-then-Q and P to conclude with the sentence Q is called modus ponens. It's a pretty fundamental "inference rule".

There's a similar rule which goes from if-P-then-Q and not-Q to the conclusion not-P. Take the example "If he didn't rob the bank then he shouldn't have any money. But he has a lot of money. So he robbed the bank." It's a valid inference. (But an important note: It may be an unsound argument! Whether it's sound or not depends on whether it's true that he has a lot of money, and it also depends on whether "If he didn't rob the bank then he shouldn't have any money." Maybe he got the money some other way.) This inference rule is called modus tollens.

Now this is all mathematical. It deals with certainty. Everything we've said is true or false. To relate this to statistics, we need to think about probabilities.
