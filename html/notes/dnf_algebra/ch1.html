Introduction to Groups

1.1 Basic Axioms and Examples

Groups are defined by an associative binary operation on some set, which has an identity and inverse. A group is abelian if the operation commutes.

Examples are vector spaces under addition, \(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}\), and we introduce the multiplicative group of integers mod n, \((\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times\). This later by definition only includes the integers wich have a multiplicative inverse mod n. We also introduce the direct product group \(A\times B\).

We derive some basic facts about groups. The identity is unique, which you can see by multiplying two identities together. Inverses are unique which you see by multiplying one element by two inverses.

The inverse of the inverse is the original. Because if you mutliply it times the inverse you get the identity.

The inverse reverse distributes just by multiplying this in a direct check.

Also we introduce the notation \(x^{-n}\) for the nth application of the inverse.

We define the order of x as the least power which yields the identity, \(|x|\).

We define the group table as that which sumarizes all group products of two elemenst.

1.2 Dihedral Groups

\(D_{2n}\) is the group of symmetric rigid motions of a polygon of n vertices. There are n rotations including the trivial one, generated by the smallest counter-clockwise rotational symmetry r. s is the reflection through the vertex labeled 1 and the origin.

We show some basic facts, like that the powers of r are distinct up to n, and |s|=2, and the reflection is not any rotation. The first interesting fact is that \(rs = sr^{-1}\) and in general \(r^i s = sr^{-i}\).

Generators and Relations

A set generates a group if every group element is some combination in the set. A presentation is a set of elements and relations among them, such that all other relations may be derived. The dihedral group is generated by r, s and is presented by the relations \(r^n=s^2=1, rs=sr^{-1}\).

1.3 Symmetric Groups

We denote by \(S_n\) the group of symmetries on 1, 2, ..., n. A cycle is the permutation \(a_1, ..., a_m\). Any permutation can be written as a product of disjoint cycles by chasing their mappings. Disjoint cycles don't "interact" so they commute.

\(Q_8 = \{\pm 1, \pm i, \pm j, \pm k\}\) with \(ij=k\) and \(ji=-k\) and so on.

1.6 Homomorphisms and Isomorphisms

A homomorphism is a map which respects the group operation--you get the same thing if you map and then operate, or operate then map. An isomorphism is a homomorphism that's bijective. Isomorphic groups, written \(A\cong B\) are basically the same up to relabeling.
