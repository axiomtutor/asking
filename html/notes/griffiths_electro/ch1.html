1.2.6 Product Rules

The generalized derivatives above satisfy many of the familiar derivative rules. Some which are not completely familiar are:
Gradient of dot $$\nabla(\vec{A}\cdot\vec{B}) = \vec{A}\times (\nabla \times \vec{B}) + \vec{B}\times (\nabla\times \vec{A}) + $$ $$(\vec{A}\cdot\nabla)\vec{B}+(\vec{B}\cdot\nabla)\vec{A}$$ Div of function $$\nabla \cdot(f\vec{A}) = f(\nabla\cdot\vec{A})+\vec{A}\cdot(\nabla f)$$ Div of cross $$\vec{B}\cdot(\nabla\times \vec{A})-\vec{A}\cdot(\nabla\times\vec{B})$$ Curl of function $$\nabla\times(f\vec{A})=f(\nabla\times\vec{A})-\vec{A}\times(\nabla f)$$ Curl of cross $$\nabla\times\vec{A}\times\vec{B} = (\vec{B}\cdot\nabla)\vec{A}-(\vec{A}\cdot\nabla)\vec{B}+\vec{A}(\nabla\cdot\vec{B}-\vec{B}(\nabla\cdot\vec{A}))$$

1.2.7 Second Derivatives

The Laplacian is the div of grad. It is also sometimes defined as the negative second term in $$\nabla\times(\nabla\times \vec{v}) = \nabla(\nabla\cdot\vec{v})-\nabla^2\vec{v}$$

The curl of a grad, and the div of a curl, are always 0.

1.3 Integral Calculus

1.3.1 Line, Surface, and Volume Integrals

We write line integrals as $$\int_\vec{a}^\vec{b}\vec{v}\cdot d\vec{\ell}$$ in general and over a closed path $$\oint\vec{v}\cdot d\vec{\ell}$$

A surface integral is $$\int_S\vec{v}\cdot d\vec{a}$$ and for a closed outward oriented surface $$\oint \vec{v}\cdot d\vec{a}$$

A scalar volume integral is $$\int_VT\ d\tau$$ and a vector volume integral is $$\int\vec{v}\ d\tau = \int(v_x\hat{x}+v_y\hat{y}+v_z\hat{z})\ d\tau$$

1.3.2 The FTC

1.3.3 The FTC for Gradients

$$\int_\vec{a}^\vec{b}(\nabla T)\cdot d\vec{\ell} = T(\vec{b})-T(\vec{a})$$

1.3.4 The FTC for Div

1.3.5 The FTC for Curls

$$\int_S(\nabla\times\vec{v})\cdot d\vec{a}=\oint_P\vec{v}\cdot d\vec{\ell}$$

1.4 Curvilinear Coordinates

1.4.1 Spherical Coordinates

$$x = r\sin\theta\cos\phi, \quad y = r\sin\theta\sin\phi, \quad z=r\cos\theta$$ $$\hat{r} = \sin\theta\cos\phi\hat{x}+\sin\theta\sin\phi\hat{y}+\cos\theta\hat{z}$$ $$\hat\theta = -\sin\phi\hat x + \cos\phi\hat y$$

For a change in \(\hat r\) there is a one-to-one corresponding change in \(d \vec\ell\). $$d\ell_r = dr$$ but for a change in \(\theta\) the change scales with r, $$d\ell_\theta = r\ d\theta$$ and for a fixed \(r, \theta\) the change in \(\ell\) corresponding to a change in \(\varphi\) is $$d\ell_\phi = r\sin\theta d\phi$$

From this we have $$d\vec{\ell} = d\hat{r} + r\ d\hat{\theta} + r\sin\phi\ d\hat{\phi}$$ We can also analyze derivative-like calculations in spherical coordinates. Without proof or explanation we use $$\nabla T = \frac{\partial T}{\partial r}\hat{r}+\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial T}{\partial \theta}\hat{\theta} + \frac{1}{r\sin\theta}\frac{\partial T}{\partial \phi}\hat\phi$$ $$\nabla\cdot\vec{v} = \frac{1}{r^2}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r^2 v_r) + \frac{1}{r\sin\theta}\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}(\sin \theta v_\theta) + \frac{1}{r\sin\theta}\frac{\partial v_\phi}{\partial \phi}$$ $$\nabla\times\vec{v} = \frac{1}{r\sin\theta}\left[\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\left(\sin \theta v_\phi-\frac{\partial v_\theta}{\partial \phi}\right)\right]\hat{r} + \frac{1}{r}\left[\frac{1}{\sin\theta}\frac{\partial v_r}{\partial \phi}-\frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r v_\phi)\right]\hat \theta + \frac 1 r \left[\frac\partial{\partial r}(rv_\theta) - \frac{\partial v_r}{\partial \theta}\right]\hat\phi$$

1.5.2 The Dirac Delta

By hook or by crook, we learn about the Dirac delta function which is really a generalized function. In some sense it is the limit of the following family of functions which has area 1: $$ \delta_n(x) = \begin{cases} 0 && \text{ if } |x|>\frac{1}{2n}\\ n && \text{ otherwise } \end{cases}$$ The limit of this sequence, were it a function, could be understood as representing an instantaneous leap. For instance if some object experienced a collision, its force over time graph would sharply spike at the monent of collision. Importantly, the integral of this function is 1 on any interval containing 0 and is 0 on any other interval. This corresponds to the object which received the bang, now having acquired some speed (1 unit).

The formalism surrounding the integral of the delta function takes a little getting used to. The important properties are, for any nice function, $$ \int_D \delta(x)f(x)\ dx = f(0), \quad 0\in D $$ and is 0 on any domain not containing 0. With analogous constraints $$ \int_D \delta(x-a)f(x)\ dx = f(a) $$

1.5.3 Three-D Dirac Delta

In the 3-D case the delta function is defined to give infinity at the origin and 0 elsewhere, or more precisely $$ \delta^3(\vec{v}) = \delta(x)\delta(y)\delta(z) $$ and so $$ \int_D f(\vec{v})\delta^3(\vec{v}-\vec{a})\ d\tau $$

Importantly $$ \nabla \cdot \left(\frac{\hat{\mathcal{R}}}{\mathcal{R}^2}\right) = 4\pi\delta^3(\vec{\mathcal{R}}) $$ and $$ \nabla \cdot \left(\frac{1}{\mathcal{R}}\right) = - \frac{\hat{\mathcal{R}}}{\mathcal{R}^2} $$ and $$ \nabla^2 \frac{1}{\mathcal{R}} = -4\pi\delta^3(\vec{\mathcal{R}}) $$
