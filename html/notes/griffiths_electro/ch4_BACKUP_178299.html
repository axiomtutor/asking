4. Electric Fields in Matter

4.1. Polarization

4.1.1. Diaelectrics

Dialectrics are insulators, matter with charges fixed to them. Still the fields they generate can be distorted: stretching and rotating.

4.1.2. Induced Dipoles

One way to cause stretching is to put a charge near an electrically neutral atom. This will push the positive protons in one direction and the electrons in the other, creating a dipole. For reasonable strengths of the field acting on the dipole, the effect is proportionate to the strength of the field, i.e. $$ \vec p = \alpha\vec E $$ We call \(\alpha\) the atomic polarizability and it depends on the structure of the atom in question.

4.1.3. Alignment of Polar Molecules

Some molecules, like water, have a built-in permanent dipole. When in the presence of a constant electric field the net force is zero since the force on the positive charge balances that one the negative. However there is a torque force $$ \vec N = \vec r_+\times \vec F_+ + \vec r_- \times \vec F_- $$

The center of the dipole moment will be the "lever arm" and so using \(\vec d/2\) and \(-\vec d/2\) as the the lever arms in the torque equation and exchanging \(\vec F = q\vec E\), we get $$ \vec N = q\vec d\times \vec E = \vec p\times \vec E $$

Problem 6

A perfect dipole is situated a distance z above an infinite grounded conducting plane, making an angle \(\theta\). Find the torque on the dipole. If the dipole is free to rotate, in what orientation will it come to rest?

This problem is similar enough to the infinite conducting plane near a point charge, in which we used the method of imagines because the direct approach was too difficult. But this time there's an assymmetry. The head of the dipole indicates a direction associated with the positive charge and so on that side, attracts negative charge on the plane; vice versa for the tail.

To try to imagine an image which satisfies the same boundary conditions, you could imagine the reflection of the given dipole across the plane. But since we need positive charges near the tail, like in the plane, we need to flip the orientation of the dipole. Let's confirm that in fact the boundary conditions are the same, which is to say that the potential through the plane is the same.

By the argument from last time, we again know that the potential is 0 through the plane in the conductor. So we need it to be 0 again in the case of images. In this case, at any point on the plane we can compute the field due to the dipoles, sum them, and we will see that the field points vertically, i.e. orthogonal to the plane. Thus if we brought a point charge in from infinity, along the plane, the potential would be 0 throughout its path. They indeed satify the same boundary conditions.

So now we need to find the force exerted on the dipole by the image dipole. This is as in the previous section, and can be found by taking the gradient of \(-V_{dip} = -(kp\cos\theta)/r^2\). Then use \(\vec N = \vec p \times \vec E\).

4.1.4. Polarization

From the previous discussions we can now summarize that, in a dialectric, if the atoms are neutral then we can induce a charge and the induced dipoles line up with the field. If the atoms form a natural dipole they still line up with the field.

In any large body that is polarized we can define \(\vec P\) the dipole moment per unit volume.

In the next several sections we will now forget the field that caused polarization. Instead we will consider the field that now results from the polarized body.

4.2. The Field of a Polarized Object

4.2.1. Bound Charges

The obvious trick in light of defining \(\vec P\) is to find the field caused by a polarized body by integrating a bunch of small dipoles. Potential has the simplest form in terms of a dipole, so we use that. $$ V(\vec r) = k\int_V\frac{\vec{P}(\vec r')\cdot \hat{ \mathcal{R}}}{\mathcal{R}^2} \ d\tau'$$ But using some obscure vector analysis integration by parts, we end up with the result that: the potential and the field of a polarized object is the same as what would be produced by a volume charge density defined by $$\rho_b = -\nabla\cdot\vec P$$ plus a surface charge density defined by $$\sigma_b = \vec P\cdot \hat n$$ We call these the bound charges.
