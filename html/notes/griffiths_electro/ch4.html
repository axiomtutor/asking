4. Electric Fields in Matter

4.1. Polarization

4.1.1. Diaelectrics

Dialectrics are insulators, matter with charges fixed to them. Still the fields they generate can be distorted: stretching and rotating.

4.1.2. Induced Dipoles

One way to cause stretching is to put a charge near an electrically neutral atom. This will push the positive protons in one direction and the electrons in the other, creating a dipole. For reasonable strengths of the field acting on the dipole, the effect is proportionate to the strength of the field, i.e. $$ \vec p = \alpha\vec E $$ We call \(\alpha\) the atomic polarizability and it depends on the structure of the atom in question.

The picture complicates when we discuss molecules because their structure can be quite complex. In particular they can polarize more readily in one direction than another. We may apply a field in each direction, x, y, z and measure the resulting polarizability. In general any field \(\vec E = \langle E_x,E_y,E_z\rangle\) results in a polarization in any of the different directions \(p_x,p_y,p_z\). This is summarized in a polarizability tensor related to the system of equations $$ p_x = \alpha_{xx}E_x + \alpha_{xy}E_y + \alpha_{xz}E_z $$ $$ p_y = \alpha_{yz}E_x + \alpha_{yy}E_y + \alpha_{yz}E_z $$ $$ p_z = \alpha_{zx}E_x + \alpha_{zy}E_y + \alpha_{zz}E_z $$

The induced dipole moment of an atom is, as we said, pro- portional to the external field. This is a “rule of thumb,” not a fundamental law, and it is easy to concoct exceptions--in theory. Although this is the strength of the field genderated by the electron cloud at equilibrium, it is at equilibrium at a distance d in a dipole where p=qd. And this shows p is proportionate to the square-root of E.

Problem 4

A point charge q is situated a large distance r from a neutral atom of polarizability \(\alpha\). Find the force of attraction between them.

First we imagine that the charge q creates a dipole in the atom. Once that dipole exists, we imagine it exerts a force on the charge q. To know the moment of the dipole that the charge creates, we first consider the field generated by the charge, \(E = kq/r^2\). Then use \(\vec p = \alpha \vec E\).

Now that we know the dipole moment we want to know the strength of the field it generates on the point charge q. Since one can be sure the dipole axis is on the line between the charge and the atom, we only need \(E_r = \frac{2kp\cos\theta}{r^3}\).

4.1.3. Alignment of Polar Molecules

Some molecules, like water, have a built-in permanent dipole. When in the presence of a constant electric field the net force is zero since the force on the positive charge balances that one the negative. However there is a torque force $$ \vec N = \vec r_+\times \vec F_+ + \vec r_- \times \vec F_- $$

The center of the dipole moment will be the "lever arm" and so using \(\vec d/2\) and \(-\vec d/2\) as the the lever arms in the torque equation and exchanging \(\vec F = q\vec E\), we get $$ \vec N = q\vec d\times \vec E = \vec p\times \vec E $$

Problem 6

A perfect dipole is situated a distance z above an infinite grounded conducting plane, making an angle \(\theta\). Find the torque on the dipole. If the dipole is free to rotate, in what orientation will it come to rest?

This problem is similar enough to the infinite conducting plane near a point charge, in which we used the method of imagines because the direct approach was too difficult. But this time there's an assymmetry. The head of the dipole indicates a direction associated with the positive charge and so on that side, attracts negative charge on the plane; vice versa for the tail.

To try to imagine an image which satisfies the same boundary conditions, you could imagine the reflection of the given dipole across the plane. But since we need positive charges near the tail, like in the plane, we need to flip the orientation of the dipole. Let's confirm that in fact the boundary conditions are the same, which is to say that the potential through the plane is the same.

By the argument from last time, we again know that the potential is 0 through the plane in the conductor. So we need it to be 0 again in the case of images. In this case, at any point on the plane we can compute the field due to the dipoles, sum them, and we will see that the field points vertically, i.e. orthogonal to the plane. Thus if we brought a point charge in from infinity, along the plane, the potential would be 0 throughout its path. They indeed satify the same boundary conditions.

So now we need to find the force exerted on the dipole by the image dipole. This is as in the previous section, and can be found by taking the gradient of \(-V_{dip} = -(kp\cos\theta)/r^2\). Then use \(\vec N = \vec p \times \vec E\).

4.1.4. Polarization

From the previous discussions we can now summarize that, in a dialectric, if the atoms are neutral then we can induce a charge and the induced dipoles line up with the field. If the atoms form a natural dipole they still line up with the field.

In any large body that is polarized we can define \(\vec P\) the dipole moment per unit volume.

In the next several sections we will now forget the field that caused polarization. Instead we will consider the field that now results from the polarized body.

4.2. The Field of a Polarized Object

4.2.1. Bound Charges

The obvious trick in light of defining \(\vec P\) is to find the field caused by a polarized body by integrating a bunch of small dipoles. Potential has the simplest form in terms of a dipole, so we use that. $$ V(\vec r) = k\int_V\frac{\vec{P}(\vec r')\cdot \hat{ \mathcal{R}}}{\mathcal{R}^2} \ d\tau'$$ But using some obscure vector analysis integration by parts, we end up with the result that: the potential and the field of a polarized object is the same as what would be produced by a volume charge density defined by $$\rho_b = -\nabla\cdot\vec P$$ plus a surface charge density defined by $$\sigma_b = \vec P\cdot \hat n$$ We call these the bound charges.

Example 2

A uniformly polarized sphere

If a sphere is uniformly polarized meaning that all of the dipoles point in the same direction with the same strength, call the unit polarization through the material \(\vec P\). Since it is constant the gradient, and therefore the equivalent charge density, is zero.

If we orient our coordinates so that the dipole points along the z-axis then in the usual coordinates \(\sigma_b = P\cos\theta\) which means that along the surface of the sphere, the equivalent charge density is weakest at the sides and strongest at the ends.

To find the potential due to this configuration, we can use $$ V = k\int_\infty^{\hat r_0}\frac{\rho}{r}d\tau $$ where there is a separation of the integral between the region outside and inside the sphere.

As one might start to guess based on the patterns of previous results, in this case when the calculations are carried to completion, we find that outside the sphere the potential is just as if a single perfect dipole were located at the center of the sphere. One only has to equate the dipole density with a single dipole divided by the volume of the sphere.

Problem 10

Calculate the bound charges if \(\vec P(\vec r) = c\vec r\) in a sphere of radius R. Find the field inside and out.

This time there is no preferred orientation of the axes since the dipole density is spherically symmetric. From the geometry one would expect that there would be a relative weakness of negative tails in the center and strength of positive charges at the surface. You might then guess that this acts as a spherically symmetric charge distribution at every shell, growing in strength outward.

$$ \sigma_b = \vec P \cdot \hat n = P = cR$$ $$ \rho_b = -\nabla \cdot \vec P $$ For the latter we use the formula $$ \nabla \cdot \vec v = \frac{1}{r^2}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r^2v_r) + \frac{1}{r\sin\theta}\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}(\sin\theta v_\theta) + \frac{1}{r\sin\theta}\frac{\partial v_\phi}{\partial \phi} $$ where \(\vec v = \vec r = v_r \hat r + v_\theta \hat \theta + v_\phi \hat \phi\) and so \(v_r = r\) whereas all other component functions are 0. So \(\rho_b = -c\frac{1}{r^2}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r^2(r)) = -3c\).

Outside the sphere the surface charge density is like a point charge with magnitude \(cR\cdot 4\pi R^2\). \(\rho_b\) also acts like a point charge with magnitude \(-3c\cdot \frac 4 3 \pi R^3\).

Problem 13

A long cylinder of radius a carries uniform polarization \(\vec P\) perpendicular to its axis. Find the electric field inside the cylinder and show that the field outside can be expressed as $$ \frac{a^2}{2\epsilon_0 s^2}[2(\vec P\cdot \hat s)\hat s - \vec P] $$

We can use Gauss's law to get the field at any point in the interior due first to the positive and then the negative.

These two fields are each parameterized by vectors eminating from the centers of the two cylinders. For a point outside the cylinder, you can regard the two imagined cylinders as lines of charge to derive the given formula.

4.3 The Electric Displacement

4.3.1 Gauss's Law in Dielectrics

We now know how a charge can induce a dipole in a dielectric, and it produces what we call the bound charge \(\rho_b\). If we regard every other charge as the "free charge" \(\rho_f\) then using \(\epsilon_0\nabla \cdot \vec E = \rho = \rho_b+\rho_f = -\nabla\cdot \vec P + \rho_f\) we can solve for $$\rho_f = \nabla\cdot(\epsilon_0\vec E + \vec P) $$ Note that here we take only the volumetric charge density strictly inside the dialectric so that the surface charge generates no field there. Also note that \(\vec E\) is the total field. The stuff in parentheses we denote by \(\vec D\) and call it the electric displacement.

Suppose a wire of charge runs through a rubber tube. Find the free charge.

We first fit a Gaussian surface which is a cylinder passing through any point inside the rubber. By symmetry we can first infer that the polarization is symmetric and then infer that the total field is symmetric, so the electric displacement must be too. This justifies using the Gauss's law approach and the rest is simple.

Problem 4.15

If a spherical shell of inner radius a and outer radius b has polarization defined by \(\vec P = \frac k r \hat r \). Find the electric field in the three regions by two methods.

a) Locate all the bound charge and use Gauss's law.

b) Find the electric displacement and then get \(\vec E\).

a) $$ \sigma_b = \vec P \cdot \hat n = \pm k/r $$ at each point on the outer and inner surface. It is positive on the outer, negative on the inner. For points inside the material

$$ \rho_b = -\nabla\cdot \vec P = -\frac 1 {r^2} \left(\frac{\partial }{\partial r}r^2P_r \right) $$ $$ = -\frac 1 {r^2} \left( \frac \partial {\partial r} r^2 k/r \right) = -\frac 1 {r^2} k$$

To confirm that this makes sense let's see that the total charge is 0. On the outer surface the net charge is \(4\pi b^2 k/b\) and on the inner surface \(-4\pi a^2k/a \). And throughout the material \( \int_a^b (-k/r^2) (4\pi r^2) \ dr = -4\pi k (b-a) \). Since this sums to zero we may be on the right track.

From here we now just have a system of charges and can solve this using the techniques of before. To address part (b) we use the equation \(\oint \vec D \cdot d\vec a = Q_{f_{enc}} \) but the charge here is the free charge ... and in this problem there is none. So the integral over \(\vec D\) is zero entailing \(\vec D = \vec 0\). So from \(\vec D = \epsilon_0 \vec D + \vec P\) we infer \(\vec D = -\frac {\vec P}{\epsilon_0} \).

4.3.2 A Deceptive Parallel

It may seem like the displacement is just like an electric field, but importantly it is not guaranteed to be conservative. In particular the curl is not always zero, but since the curl of the electric field is, then we can say $$ \nabla\times \vec D = \nabla\times\vec P $$ Because displacement is not conservative, it has no equivalent to a "potential". The integral must be evaluated using direct calculations rather than the fundamental theorem of line integrals or other threoms that deepend on conservatism.

Boundary Conditions

The equation $$\int D\cdot d\vec a = Q_f$$ tells us the discontinuity in the component perpendicular to an interface. $$D^{\perp}_{above} - D^{\perp}_{below} = \sigma_f$$

The argument for the above goes exactly the same as it did for the electric field, using a thin Gaussian pillbox. On the other hand, the previous argument for the parallel component no longer applies. For the electric field we argued that the parallel component must be zero otherwise our system would not be in equilibrium. Here charges are not free to flow and that no longer is convincing.

But because we know the field will still have no parallel component, we can say that the difference in the displacement is the difference in the polarization. $$ \vec D_{above}^{||} - \vec D_{below}^{||} = \vec P_{above}^{||}-\vec P_{below}^{||} $$

4.4 Linear Dielectrics

4.4.1 Susceptibility, Permitivity, Dielectric Constant

For many substances $$ \vec P = \epsilon_0\chi_e \vec E $$ where \(\chi_e\) is the electric susceptibility of the medium and is dimensionless (the dimensions are accounted for in the permitivity of free space). When this approximation is valid we call the material a linear dielectric.

From this definition we gain an obvious simplification of the displacement $$ \vec D = \epsilon_0(1+\chi_e)\vec E = \epsilon\vec E $$ where we call \(\epsilon\) the permitivity of the material. We also call the following the relative permitivity: $$ \epsilon_r = \frac \epsilon {\epsilon_0} = 1+\chi_e $$

Example 4.5

A metal sphere of radius a carries charge Q. It is surrounded, out to radius b, by linear dialectric material of permativity \(\epsilon\). Find the potential at the center.

Traditionally to get the potential we want the field. Here to get the field, we would perhaps want to find the bound charge of the dielectric as we have in the past. But the polarization is first due to \(\vec E\) and we've come full-circle.

Nonetheless we can calculate \(\vec D\) using the free charge. We fit a Gaussian surface around it and use the diplacement Gauss's law. $$ \vec D = \frac Q {4\pi r^2}\hat r\ \ \ \ \text{ for \(r>a\)} $$ And of course inside a metal sphere the field is zero.

Now \(\vec E = \vec D/\epsilon\) in the material and \(\vec E = \vec D/\epsilon_0\) in free space. With this we can next find the potential by bringing a particle in from infinity to the center. Naturally we divide the path into those parts outside the dielectric, the part inside, and the part inside the sphere. Of course the part inside the sphere contributes nothing to potential. $$ V = - \int_\infty^a\vec E\cdot d\vec \ell = -\frac 1 \epsilon_0 \int_\infty^b\frac {kQ} {r^2}dr - \frac 1 \epsilon_0 \int_b^a \frac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon r^2}dr $$

You might suspect that since linear dielectrics are proportional to the field then the displacement is then conservative like the field. Unfortunately the equation is only true in most of space, and not in all of space--but this exception is enough to make the field not conservative.

In particular at the interface between one dielectric and another, the integral of a closed path around that interface will make \(\vec E\) vanish but \(\vec P\) will not. What's interesting about this is that the displacement would be conservative if we further required that the material fill all of space.

For example, if a free charge q is embedded in a large dielectric, the field it produces is \(\vec E = \vec D/\epsilon = \frac{1}{4\pi \epsilon}\frac{q}{r^2}\hat r\).

Example 4.6

A parallel plate capacitor is filled with a dielectric with constant \(\epsilon_r\). What effect does this have on the capacitance?

If \(\vec E_0\) is the field without the dielectric, $$\vec E = \frac 1 {\epsilon_r} \vec E_0 $$ similar to what we saw just above with a point charge. This immediately implies the relationship between potential differences. From \(C = Q/V\) we get \(C = \epsilon_r C_{vac}\).

4.2 Boundary Value Problems with Linear Dialectrics

In a homogeneous isotropic linear dialectric, the bound charge density is proportional to the free charge density $$ \rho_b = -\nabla\cdot\vec P = -\nabla \cdot \left( \epsilon_0\frac{\chi_e}{\epsilon} \vec D \right) = -\left(\frac{\chi_e}{1+\chi_e}\right)\rho_f $$

The first equation above is because of the definition of the bound charge. The second is due to linearity. The third is the theorem we found at the end of the discussion of unit polarization.

We earlier saw that \(D^\perp_{above}-D^{\perp}_{below} = \sigma_f\) which tells us that $$\epsilon_{above}E^{\perp}_{above}-\epsilon_{below}E^{\perp} = \sigma_f$$

We can rephrase this in terms of the potential $$\epsilon_{above}\frac{\partial V_{above}}{\partial n} - \epsilon_{below}\frac{\partial V_{below}}{\partial n} = -\sigma_{f}$$

But we know the potential is continuous so that \(V_{above}=V_{below}\).

What did we learn here? The potential being continuous is really looking at two sides of a function at a corner. The potential is, say, an increasing function just above the surface and decreasing just below. So you expect the partial derivatives to not be equal, yet here there seems like an implied ellipsis suggesting that the partial derivatives are equal.
