4. Electric Fields in Matter

4.1. Polarization

4.1.1. Diaelectrics

Dialectrics are insulators, matter with charges fixed to them. Still the fields they generate can be distorted: stretching and rotating.

4.1.2. Induced Dipoles

One way to cause stretching is to put a charge near an electrically neutral atom. This will push the positive protons in one direction and the electrons in the other, creating a dipole. For reasonable strengths of the field acting on the dipole, the effect is proportionate to the strength of the field, i.e. $$ \vec p = \alpha\vec E $$ We call \(\alpha\) the atomic polarizability and it depends on the structure of the atom in question.

The picture complicates when we discuss molecules because their structure can be quite complex. In particular they can polarize more readily in one direction than another. We may apply a field in each direction, x, y, z and measure the resulting polarizability. In general any field \(\vec E = \langle E_x,E_y,E_z\rangle\) results in a polarization in any of the different directions \(p_x,p_y,p_z\). This is summarized in a polarizability tensor related to the system of equations $$ p_x = \alpha_{xx}E_x + \alpha_{xy}E_y + \alpha_{xz}E_z $$ $$ p_y = \alpha_{yz}E_x + \alpha_{yy}E_y + \alpha_{yz}E_z $$ $$ p_z = \alpha_{zx}E_x + \alpha_{zy}E_y + \alpha_{zz}E_z $$

Problem 3

The induced dipole moment of an atom is, as we said, pro- portional to the external field. This is a “rule of thumb,” not a fundamental law, and it is easy to concoct exceptions--in theory. Suppose, for example, the charge density of the electron cloud were proportional to the distance from the center, out to a radius R. To what power of E would p be proportional in that case? Find the condition on ρ(r) such that proportionality will hold in the weak-field limit.

We assume the density is proportionate to r, $$ \rho(r) = cr, \quad r < R $$ This might require explicit calculation of the field and dipole moment. Since we're still talking about a dipole in equilibrium the field must balance with the restoring force. So the field we want is equal the field generated by the electron at the point of the proton. The easiest calculation of this field given the charge distribution is with Gauss's law, where we fit a sphere centered at the center of the electron and passing through the proton. We will need the charge within that sphere.

To get Q we integrate over spherical shells each of volume approximately \(4\pi r'^2\ dr'\) so that the net charge is $$ \int_0^r \rho(r') 4\pi r'^2 \ dr' = 4\pi\int_0^r cr' \cdot r'^2\ dr' $$ $$ = 4c\pi [r'^4/4]_0^r = c\pi r^4 $$

Gauss's law tells us \((4\pi r^2 )E = Q/\epsilon_0\) so $$ E = \frac{cr^2}{4} $$

Although this is the strength of the field genderated by the electron cloud at equilibrium, it is at equilibrium at a distance d in a dipole where p=qd. And this shows p is proportionate to the square-root of E.

Problem 4

A point charge q is situated a large distance r from a neutral atom of polarizability \(\alpha\). Find the force of attraction between them.

First we imagine that the charge q creates a dipole in the atom. Once that dipole exists, we imagine it exerts a force on the charge q. To know the moment of the dipole that the charge creates, we first consider the field generated by the charge, \(E = kq/r^2\). Then use \(\vec p = \alpha \vec E\).

Now that we know the dipole moment we want to know the strength of the field it generates on the point charge q. Since one can be sure the dipole axis is on the line between the charge and the atom, we only need \(E_r = \frac{2kp\cos\theta}{r^3}\).

4.1.3. Alignment of Polar Molecules

Some molecules, like water, have a built-in permanent dipole. When in the presence of a constant electric field the net force is zero since the force on the positive charge balances that one the negative. However there is a torque force $$ \vec N = \vec r_+\times \vec F_+ + \vec r_- \times \vec F_- $$

The center of the dipole moment will be the "lever arm" and so using \(\vec d/2\) and \(-\vec d/2\) as the the lever arms in the torque equation and exchanging \(\vec F = q\vec E\), we get $$ \vec N = q\vec d\times \vec E = \vec p\times \vec E $$

Problem 5

\(\vec p_1, \vec p_2\) are perfect dipoles a distance r apart, directed orthogonally. \(\vec p_2\) is directed away from \(\vec p_1\) What is the torque on \(\vec p_2\) due to \(\vec p_1\)? (More precisely, the torque on one about its own center.)

We just find the field due to \(\vec p_1\) and use \(\vec N = \vec p_2\times \vec E\).

Problem 6

A perfect dipole is situated a distance z above an infinite grounded conducting plane, making an angle \(\theta\). Find the torque on the dipole. If the dipole is free to rotate, in what orientation will it come to rest?

This problem is similar enough to the infinite conducting plane near a point charge, in which we used the method of imagines because the direct approach was too difficult. But this time there's an assymmetry. The head of the dipole indicates a direction associated with the positive charge and so on that side, attracts negative charge on the plane; vice versa for the tail.

To try to imagine an image which satisfies the same boundary conditions, you could imagine the reflection of the given dipole across the plane. But since we need positive charges near the tail, like in the plane, we need to flip the orientation of the dipole. Let's confirm that in fact the boundary conditions are the same, which is to say that the potential through the plane is the same.

By the argument from last time, we again know that the potential is 0 through the plane in the conductor. So we need it to be 0 again in the case of images. In this case, at any point on the plane we can compute the field due to the dipoles, sum them, and we will see that the field points vertically, i.e. orthogonal to the plane. Thus if we brought a point charge in from infinity, along the plane, the potential would be 0 throughout its path. They indeed satify the same boundary conditions.

So now we need to find the force exerted on the dipole by the image dipole. This is as in the previous section, and can be found by taking the gradient of \(-V_{dip} = -(kp\cos\theta)/r^2\). Then use \(\vec N = \vec p \times \vec E\).

Problem 7

Suppose that we drag the dipole in from infinity in some fixed orientation \(\vec d\). You can regard this as bringing the negative charge in and then the positive along the same path but stopping at a point \(\vec d\) relative to the electron--it's easiest to imagine a straight line which both particles use, but the positive particle stops early. The energy is then just the difference between the work done by the field on these two particles.

Symmetry can be useful here. We know that if the positive particle completed exactly the same path as the negative, the total work done would be zero. So although the description above is useful, let's change strategies to use the symmetry insight Bring the negative in to its position along the direction of the dipole. Now bring the positive in on exactly the same path so that they lie on top of each other--the work done by the field is now a net zero. Now push the positive charge out along \(\vec d\) to its correct location. Since \(\vec d\) is very small we can regard the field as constant along this path, and the work done is \(q\vec E\cdot \vec d\). After rearranging, you get \(\vec p\cdot \vec E\) and since this is the work done by the field, the change in potential energy is the negative of this.

4.1.4. Polarization

From the previous discussions we can now summarize that, in a dialectric, if the atoms are neutral then we can induce a charge and the induced dipoles line up with the field. If the atoms form a natural dipole they still line up with the field.

In any large body that is polarized we can define \(\vec P\) the dipole moment per unit volume.

In the next several sections we will now forget the field that caused polarization. Instead we will consider the field that now results from the polarized body.

4.2. The Field of a Polarized Object

4.2.1. Bound Charges

The obvious trick in light of defining \(\vec P\) is to find the field caused by a polarized body by integrating a bunch of small dipoles. Potential has the simplest form in terms of a dipole, so we use that. $$ V(\vec r) = k\int_V\frac{\vec{P}(\vec r')\cdot \hat{ \mathcal{R}}}{\mathcal{R}^2} \ d\tau'$$ But using some obscure vector analysis integration by parts, we end up with the result that: the potential and the field of a polarized object is the same as what would be produced by a volume charge density defined by $$\rho_b = -\nabla\cdot\vec P$$ plus a surface charge density defined by $$\sigma_b = \vec P\cdot \hat n$$ We call these the bound charges.

Example 2

A uniformly polarized sphere

If a sphere is uniformly polarized meaning that all of the dipoles point in the same direction with the same strength, call the unit polarization through the material \(\vec P\). Since it is constant the gradient, and therefore the equivalent charge density, is zero.

If we orient our coordinates so that the dipole points along the z-axis then in the usual coordinates \(\sigma_b = P\cos\theta\) which means that along the surface of the sphere, the equivalent charge density is weakest at the sides and strongest at the ends.

To find the potential due to this configuration, we can use $$ V = k\int_\infty^{\hat r_0}\frac{\rho}{r}d\tau $$ where there is a separation of the integral between the region outside and inside the sphere.

As one might start to guess based on the patterns of previous results, in this case when the calculations are carried to completion, we find that outside the sphere the potential is just as if a single perfect dipole were located at the center of the sphere. One only has to equate the dipole density with a single dipole divided by the volume of the sphere.

Problem 10

Calculate the bound charges if \(\vec P(\vec r) = c\vec r\) in a sphere of radius R. Find the field inside and out.

This time there is no preferred orientation of the axes since the dipole density is spherically symmetric. From the geometry one would expect that there would be a relative weakness of negative tails in the center and strength of positive charges at the surface. You might then guess that this acts as a spherically symmetric charge distribution at every shell, growing in strength outward.

$$ \sigma_b = \vec P \cdot \hat n = P = cR$$ $$ \rho_b = -\nabla \cdot \vec P $$ For the latter we use the formula $$ \nabla \cdot \vec v = \frac{1}{r^2}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r^2v_r) + \frac{1}{r\sin\theta}\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}(\sin\theta v_\theta) + \frac{1}{r\sin\theta}\frac{\partial v_\phi}{\partial \phi} $$ where \(\vec v = \vec r = v_r \hat r + v_\theta \hat \theta + v_\phi \hat \phi\) and so \(v_r = r\) whereas all other component functions are 0. So \(\rho_b = -c\frac{1}{r^2}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r^2(r)) = -3c\).

Outside the sphere the surface charge density is like a point charge with magnitude \(cR\cdot 4\pi R^2\). \(\rho_b\) also acts like a point charge with magnitude \(-3c\cdot \frac 4 3 \pi R^3\).

Problem 13

A long cylinder of radius a carries uniform polarization \(\vec P\) perpendicular to its axis. Find the electric field inside the cylinder and show that the field outside can be expressed as $$ \frac{a^2}{2\epsilon_0 s^2}[2(\vec P\cdot \hat s)\hat s - \vec P] $$

Because the unit polarization is uniform we know that the volumetric bound charge is 0, as it's the negative gradient of polarization. The surface charge is the same as if there were two copies of the cylinder, one positive and the other negative, shifted by some small \(\vec d\). We can use Gauss's law to get the field at any point in the interior due first to the positive and then the negative.

These two fields are each parameterized by vectors eminating from the centers of the two cylinders. Some moderate vector calculus shows that the sum of the two fields, involving a difference of the relative displacements, is equivalent to \(\vec d\) and you get a very simple solution in terms of the polarization.

For a point outside the cylinder, you can regard the two imagined cylinders as lines of charge to derive the given formula.
